The 40-Hour Jacket:
What It Takes to Make
a Kvastram Piece

By Arjun Sharma, Founder Â· Photography by Kavya Nair

It begins at 14,000 feet above sea level, in the high-altitude plateau of Ladakh, where the Changthangi goat grazes on sparse winter grass. In spring, when the animal naturally sheds its coat, local herders gently comb the fine undercoat â€” the softest fiber in the world â€” by hand. This is where your Kvastram piece begins.

From Ladakh, the raw fiber travels to Kashmir, where it is sorted by hand under natural light. The finest fibers â€” as thin as 12 microns, six times thinner than a human hair â€” are selected for weaving. This sorting alone can take a full day for a single kilogram of material.

"I have been weaving for 35 years. My father taught me. His father taught him. When I hold the shuttle, I am not just making a shawl â€” I am continuing a conversation across generations."

â€” Ramesh Verma, Master Weaver, Varanasi

The Loom: Where Thread Becomes Fabric

Each piece is woven on a handloom that has not fundamentally changed in 500 years. The weaver works the shuttle by hand with a rhythm built over decades. For the embroidered jacket, this weaving alone takes 80 hours across two weeks.

Then comes the embroidery. A single jacket contains approximately 40,000 individual stitches. No two are exactly alike.

Why It Costs What It Costs

40 hours of skilled labor at a fair wage. Premium pashmina silk. Hand-embroidery by a craftsperson with 35 years of experience. Natural dyes. Sustainable packaging. Carbon-offset shipping. We are not selling a jacket. We are selling 40 hours of someone's life's work, packaged in a material that will last longer than either of us.
